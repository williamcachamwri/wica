title: The Quiet Joy of Small Tools
date: 2024-03-15
summary: Why I prefer building tiny, focused utilities over large platforms.
---

There is a peculiar satisfaction in building something small.

A tool that does exactly one thing — well. No onboarding wizard, no dashboard, no
team invites. Just a single command, a clean output, and the quiet hum of a problem
solved.

## The case for small

Large platforms promise integration. Everything in one place. But that place
inevitably grows into a city, with its own traffic, zoning laws, and bureaucracy.
Small tools are different. They are huts in the woods. You walk in, you light a fire,
you leave.

### Advantages

- **Focused surface area** — fewer features means fewer bugs.
- **Composable** — small tools chain together via pipes, sockets, or simple scripts.
- **Replaceable** — when a tool outlives its usefulness, you throw it away without
  migrating a database.

## A personal example

I wrote a static site generator in 200 lines of Go. It reads a folder, renders
Markdown, and writes HTML. No config file. It doesn't even have a name. But it has
built every version of this website for the past three years.

That tool has brought me more joy than any SaaS product I have ever used.

## The hidden cost of platforms

Every platform asks for a little more of your attention. Notifications. Metrics.
"Your trial ends in 3 days." Small tools ask for nothing. They sit on your hard drive,
silent, waiting. They do not care if you use them. That silence is a kind of respect.

I will take that silence over a dashboard any day.
